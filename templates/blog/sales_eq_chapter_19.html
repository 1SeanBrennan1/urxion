Chapter 19: Do I Like You?

This chapter focuses on the importance of likability in building rapport and earning trust. Blount explains how first impressions are formed, often subconsciously, and provides practical advice on how to enhance likability through positive behaviors, communication styles, and attention focus. He cautions against "pitch slapping" and encourages salespeople to connect with stakeholders on a personal level.

Key Terms and Definitions:

  • Likability: Possessing qualities that bring about a favorable regard; being pleasant and easy to get along with.

Main Concepts:

  • First impressions are crucial in sales, and likability is the gateway to building emotional connections with stakeholders.
  • Stakeholders are more likely to engage with and trust salespeople they like.
  • Salespeople must be mindful of their behavior, communication style, and appearance to make a positive first impression.
  • Pitch slapping, or overwhelming prospects with information and features, is a common mistake that damages likability.

Supporting Evidence:

  • The story of Michelle's mattress purchase illustrates how likability can trump price and product features in a buying decision.
  • The chapter emphasizes that being perceived as likable or unlikable happens quickly, often subconsciously, and significantly impacts the sales process.
  • The "ten keys to being more likable" provide practical advice for making a positive impression.

Critical Thinking Resources:

Critical Thinking Questions:

  • How can you quickly build rapport and establish likability with a new prospect?
  • What are some common behaviors that can make a salesperson unlikable, and how can you avoid them?
  • How can you balance the need to convey information with the need to be likable and build rapport?

Supporting Points:

  • Smiling, maintaining appropriate eye contact, using a friendly tone of voice, and demonstrating genuine interest can enhance likability.
  • Active listening, asking engaging questions, and focusing on the stakeholder's needs and interests can create a positive connection.
  • Adapting your communication style to the stakeholder's personality and preferences can improve rapport and understanding.

Counterpoints:

  • Some might argue that focusing too much on being likable can make a salesperson appear insincere or desperate.
  • Salespeople might need to be assertive and challenge stakeholders, even if it risks being perceived as unlikable.
  • Likability is subjective and what one person finds likable another might not, making it difficult to control.

Rebuttals:

  • Authenticity is key to likability. Genuine interest, respect, and a sincere desire to help will shine through and create a positive impression.
  • Assertiveness and challenging stakeholders can be done respectfully and constructively without sacrificing likability.
  • While likability is subjective, there are universal behaviors and communication styles that generally create a positive impression.

Discussion Topics:

  • The role of personality and authenticity in likability.
  • The balance between being likable and being effective in challenging sales situations.
  • The ethical considerations of using techniques to enhance likability.

Argument Builder:

Supporting Points:

  • Likability is the foundation for building trust and rapport, which are essential for successful selling.
  • By focusing on the ten keys to likability, salespeople can increase their chances of making a positive first impression and creating a positive buying experience.
  • Likability enhances communication, reduces resistance, and makes it easier for stakeholders to engage with your message and consider your solutions.

Counterpoints:

  • Some might argue that likability is a superficial quality that distracts from the core value proposition of the product or service.
  • Overemphasis on likability might make salespeople appear too eager to please and compromise their negotiating power.
  • Likability can be manipulated and used to influence stakeholders unethically.

Rebuttals:

  • Likability is not superficial; it reflects genuine interest, respect, and a desire to build a connection. It enhances, not replaces, the value proposition.
  • Likability should be balanced with assertiveness and a clear understanding of your objectives. It's about building rapport, not about being a pushover.
  • While techniques can enhance likability, ethical considerations should always guide your approach. Authenticity and integrity are paramount for building trust.

Practical Application 1: Boosting Your Likability

Actionable Task 19.1: Practice the "Ten Keys to Being More Likable":

  • Steps: Review the ten keys outlined in Chapter 19 (smiling, voice tone, politeness, dress, grooming, attention focus, style, language, enthusiasm, confidence) and identify areas where you can improve.
  • Resources: Self-assessment tools, feedback from colleagues, resources on nonverbal communication, etiquette, and personal branding.
  • Obstacles/Solutions: Feeling uncomfortable with certain aspects of self-presentation? Start with small changes and gradually expand your comfort zone.
  • Timeline: Make a conscious effort to incorporate these keys into your daily interactions, both professional and personal.

Actionable Task 19.2: Avoid Pitch Slapping:

  • Steps: Resist the urge to overwhelm stakeholders with information and features. Instead, focus on asking questions, listening attentively, and tailoring your message to their specific needs and interests.
  • Resources: Active listening techniques, questioning frameworks, resources on storytelling and presentation skills.
  • Obstacles/Solutions: Stakeholder seems impatient or disengaged? Check for understanding, ask clarifying questions, and refocus the conversation on their priorities.
  • Timeline: Implement this approach in all your sales conversations and presentations.

Evaluation:

Success is measured by an increase in positive feedback from stakeholders, a greater willingness to engage in conversation, and a reduction in resistance or objections.
