Chapter 24: Do You Make Me Feel Important?

Blount explores the profound impact of making stakeholders feel valued and important. He discusses the need for significance as a powerful motivator and explains how to activate the law of reciprocity by making stakeholders feel important. The chapter provides practical strategies for recognizing and fulfilling this need, including using the stakeholder's name, remembering personal details, offering compliments, and asking for advice.

Key Terms and Definitions:

  • Significance, Need for: The most fundamental human need; the desire to feel valued, acknowledged, and important.
  • Law of Reciprocity: A social psychology principle that states that people are more likely to do something for you if you've done something for them first.

Main Concepts:

Making stakeholders feel important is crucial for building rapport, earning trust, and influencing their decisions. The need for significance is a powerful motivator that can be leveraged to create emotional connections and shape buying behavior. Salespeople can activate the law of reciprocity by making stakeholders feel important, increasing their willingness to reciprocate with engagement and commitment.

Supporting Evidence:

  • The story of Sid, impressed by the author's ability to engage the executive team: Demonstrating the power of making stakeholders feel important by knowing their names, understanding their challenges, and speaking their language.
  • Research showing that humans spend most of their conversation time talking about themselves: Highlighting the self-centered nature of human behavior and the inherent need for significance.
  • The example of Epp Wilson leveraging reciprocity by offering additional services: Illustrating how going above and beyond for customers can foster goodwill and create a sense of obligation.

Critical Thinking Resources:

Critical Thinking Questions:

  • What are some practical ways to make stakeholders feel important during sales interactions?
  • How can you leverage the law of reciprocity without appearing manipulative or transactional?
  • How can you identify and address the stakeholder's unique need for significance?

Supporting Points:

  • Using the stakeholder's name, remembering personal details, complimenting achievements, and asking for advice can make them feel valued and important.
  • Offering genuine help, going above and beyond expectations, and demonstrating a sincere desire to solve the stakeholder's problems can activate the law of reciprocity.
  • Observing nonverbal cues, listening attentively to the stakeholder's concerns, and tailoring your message to their specific needs can help you understand their need for significance.

Counterpoints:

  • Some might argue that focusing on making stakeholders feel important is manipulative and insincere.
  • Overemphasis on flattery or compliments can appear disingenuous and erode trust.
  • The law of reciprocity can be abused to create a sense of obligation or guilt, leading to resentment.

Rebuttals:

  • Making stakeholders feel important should be driven by genuine respect and a desire to build a connection, not by a desire to manipulate.
  • Sincere compliments and acknowledgments are appreciated, while excessive flattery is easily recognized as insincere.
  • The law of reciprocity should be used ethically, focusing on creating a mutually beneficial exchange of value, not on exploiting vulnerabilities.

Discussion Topics:

  • The role of authenticity and sincerity in making stakeholders feel important.
  • The ethical considerations of leveraging the law of reciprocity.
  • The challenges of balancing the need to make stakeholders feel important with the need to assert your expertise and advocate for your solution.

Argument Builder:

Supporting Points:
  • By making stakeholders feel important, salespeople can create a positive emotional connection, enhance rapport, and increase their influence.
  • Leveraging the law of reciprocity ethically can create a sense of goodwill, obligation, and a desire to collaborate, increasing the likelihood of securing commitments.
  • Understanding and addressing the stakeholder's need for significance demonstrates empathy, respect, and a customer-centric approach, leading to stronger relationships and higher win probability.
Counterpoints:
  • Some might argue that focusing too much on making stakeholders feel important can distract salespeople from the core aspects of selling, such as presenting value and closing the deal.
  • Excessive compliments or flattery can appear insincere and damage credibility.
  • Stakeholders might perceive attempts to activate the law of reciprocity as manipulative and transactional, hindering the development of a genuine relationship.
Rebuttals:
  • Making stakeholders feel important should be integrated seamlessly into the sales process, enhancing communication, building rapport, and creating a more positive buying experience.
  • Sincere compliments and acknowledgments are appreciated, while excessive flattery is easily recognized as disingenuous. Focus on genuine appreciation and respect.
  • The law of reciprocity is a natural human tendency, and it can be leveraged ethically by focusing on creating a mutually beneficial exchange of value, not on exploiting vulnerabilities or creating a sense of obligation.

Practical Application 1: Making Stakeholders Feel Valued

Actionable Task 24.1: Practice the Art of the Compliment:

Steps: Identify genuine and specific qualities or accomplishments to compliment in each stakeholder. Make your compliments sincere and avoid empty flattery.

Resources: Review your stakeholder research, observe their behavior during meetings, and listen for opportunities to offer authentic praise.

Obstacles/Solutions: Finding it difficult to find genuine compliments? Focus on their expertise, their contributions to the company, or their problem-solving skills.

Timeline: Aim to offer at least one sincere compliment to each stakeholder during your next three sales interactions.

Actionable Task 24.2: Ask for Advice and Opinions:

Steps: Show stakeholders you value their expertise by asking for their advice or opinions on relevant topics. Demonstrate that you are genuinely interested in their perspective.

Resources: Prepare specific questions related to their area of expertise or their experience with similar situations.

Obstacles/Solutions: Stakeholder seems hesitant to offer advice? Reassure them that you value their input and explain how it will help you better understand their needs.

Timeline: Incorporate this approach into your next two sales conversations.

Evaluation:

Success is measured by the stakeholder's positive response to your compliments and requests for advice, a greater willingness to share their insights, and an increased sense of trust and connection.
