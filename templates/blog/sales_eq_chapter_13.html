Chapter 13: Engagement and Micro-Commitments

This chapter focuses on the importance of securing "micro-commitments" - small, incremental agreements that build momentum and increase buy-in. Blount explains how to test stakeholder engagement, ask for micro-commitments effectively, and leverage the value bias and consistency principle to shape buyer behavior. He emphasizes the need for consistent forward progress and the danger of stalled deals.

Key Terms and Definitions:

  • Engagement: The level of interest, involvement, and commitment a stakeholder demonstrates during the sales process.
  • Micro-Commitments: Small, incremental agreements or actions that move a deal forward, building momentum and increasing win probability.

Main Concepts:

  • Engaging stakeholders and securing micro-commitments are crucial for keeping deals moving forward and preventing stalls.
  • Testing engagement involves observing emotional cues, asking for small commitments, and being willing to walk away if a stakeholder is not genuinely invested.
  • Micro-commitments leverage the value bias (people value what they invest in) and the consistency principle (people strive to be consistent with their commitments).

Supporting Evidence:

  • The story of Kurt Long securing a deal through micro-commitments: Demonstrating how small agreements can build momentum and lead to larger commitments.
  • The three purposes of testing engagement: Highlighting the importance of assessing stakeholder investment, gauging win probability, and increasing commitment through small steps.
  • The example of the heavy equipment company and the misinterpreted eagerness: Illustrating how eagerness can be misleading and the importance of testing engagement before assuming a deal is closed.

Critical Thinking Resources:

Critical Thinking Questions:

  • How can you effectively test engagement and identify whether a stakeholder is genuinely invested in the sales process?
  • What are some strategies for securing micro-commitments and building momentum towards a close?
  • How can you leverage the value bias and the consistency principle to influence stakeholder behavior?

Supporting Points:

  • Asking open-ended questions, observing nonverbal cues, and listening attentively for emotional indicators can help you gauge stakeholder engagement.
  • Framing requests as small, incremental steps, highlighting the benefits of taking action, and creating a sense of urgency can encourage micro-commitments.
  • By acknowledging and appreciating each micro-commitment, you reinforce the value bias and encourage the stakeholder to remain consistent with their actions.

Counterpoints:

  • Some might argue that focusing too much on micro-commitments can make the sales process feel overly transactional and mechanical.
  • Excessive requests for small commitments might annoy stakeholders and create a sense of pressure.
  • Relying solely on micro-commitments might neglect the importance of building genuine relationships and understanding the stakeholder's broader needs.

Rebuttals:

  • Micro-commitments should be used strategically and purposefully, not as a manipulative tactic to pressure buyers. It's about creating a collaborative and transparent sales process.
  • Framing requests thoughtfully and emphasizing the benefits of taking action can make micro-commitments feel less demanding and more like a natural progression.
  • Micro-commitments are a valuable tool for building momentum and gauging stakeholder investment, but they should be combined with a genuine focus on building relationships and understanding the buyer's needs.

Discussion Topics:

  • The balance between focusing on micro-commitments and building authentic relationships.
  • The role of trust and transparency in securing micro-commitments.
  • The ethical considerations of using psychological principles to influence stakeholder behavior.

Argument Builder:

Supporting Points:

  • Securing micro-commitments creates a sense of momentum and makes it progressively harder for stakeholders to back out of a deal.
  • By consistently testing engagement, salespeople can identify potential roadblocks early on and adjust their approach accordingly.
  • Micro-commitments leverage psychological principles that are inherent in human decision-making, making them a powerful tool for influencing outcomes.

Counterpoints:

  • Focusing too much on micro-commitments might neglect the importance of addressing broader stakeholder concerns and building long-term relationships.
  • Excessive requests for small commitments can make the sales process feel pushy and manipulative.
  • Stakeholders might perceive micro-commitments as a lack of trust and become resistant to the sales process.

Rebuttals:

  • Micro-commitments should be part of a holistic sales approach that includes building rapport, understanding needs, and addressing concerns. They are not a replacement for building relationships.
  • Framing requests thoughtfully, emphasizing the benefits of taking action, and creating a sense of collaboration can make micro-commitments feel less demanding.
  • Transparency and open communication can help address any concerns stakeholders might have about micro-commitments and build trust in the sales process.

Practical Application 1: Mastering Micro-Commitments

Actionable Task 13.1: Identify Opportunities for Micro-Commitments:

Steps: Review your sales process and identify specific points where you can ask for small commitments, such as agreeing to a next meeting, providing access to data, or introducing you to other stakeholders.

Resources: Sales process documentation, brainstorming sessions with colleagues or a sales coach.

Obstacles/Solutions: Struggling to find opportunities? Get creative and consider any action, no matter how small, that demonstrates progress and buy-in.

Timeline: Identify at least three potential micro-commitments within your sales process by the end of the week.

Actionable Task 13.2: Practice the Assumptive Ask:

Steps: When asking for micro-commitments, use confident and assumptive language, as if you expect the stakeholder to say yes. Avoid hesitant or apologetic phrasing.

Resources: Role-playing with a colleague or sales coach, scripting your assumptive asks in advance.

Obstacles/Solutions: Feeling uncomfortable being assertive? Remind yourself that you are offering value and asking for a reasonable commitment that will benefit the stakeholder.

Timeline: Practice using the assumptive ask in your next three sales conversations.

Evaluation:

Success is measured by an increase in the number of micro-commitments you secure, a reduction in stalled deals, and a more predictable and efficient sales cycle.
