Chapter 28: Do I Trust and Believe You? – The Currency of Trust

Blount explores the critical role of trust in building strong relationships and closing deals. He discusses the challenges of overcoming emotional baggage and negative perceptions of salespeople. He uses Stephen Covey's "emotional bank account" metaphor to illustrate how trust is built and eroded through consistent actions and communication. He emphasizes the importance of integrity, transparency, and being mindful of the "stage" you are always on in sales.

Key Terms and Definitions

  • Trust: A firm belief in the reliability, truth, ability, or strength of someone or something.
  • Emotional Baggage: Negative experiences and perceptions from past interactions with salespeople that can create distrust and skepticism.
  • "Emotional Bank Account": A metaphor for the level of trust and goodwill built up in a relationship.
  • Stage: A constant state of being observed and judged in sales, where every action, behavior, and communication can impact the stakeholder's perception of your trustworthiness.

Main Concepts

  • Trust is the foundation of any successful sales relationship, as it allows stakeholders to feel confident in your ability to deliver on your promises.
  • Building trust requires consistent actions, honest communication, and a genuine desire to help the stakeholder achieve their objectives.
  • Salespeople must manage their "emotional baggage" and avoid behaviors that can erode trust, such as making false promises, being unprepared, or appearing manipulative.
  • In sales, you are always "on stage," and stakeholders are constantly evaluating your trustworthiness based on your actions, behaviors, and communication.

Supporting Evidence

  • The statistic that 70% of the U.S. population believes most people can't be trusted highlights the challenge salespeople face in overcoming a prevailing sense of distrust.
  • Stephen Covey's "emotional bank account" metaphor illustrates how trust is built up through consistent deposits of positive interactions and eroded by withdrawals of negative experiences.
  • The example of a salesperson being late to a meeting without calling demonstrates how seemingly small actions can damage trust and impact perceptions of reliability.

Critical Thinking Resources

Critical Thinking Questions

  • How can you overcome the initial barrier of distrust and build a foundation of trust with new stakeholders?
  • What are some specific behaviors and actions that can erode trust, and how can you avoid them?
  • How can you use the concept of being "on stage" to manage your behavior and ensure that you are projecting trustworthiness in all your interactions?

Supporting Points

  • Active listening, demonstrating empathy, being prepared, keeping your promises, and following through on commitments can build trust.
  • Avoiding exaggeration, jargon, or manipulative tactics and being transparent and honest in your communication can enhance credibility.
  • Being mindful of your nonverbal cues, appearance, and professionalism can project confidence and competence.

Counterpoints

  • Some might argue that building trust takes too much time and that salespeople should focus on closing the deal quickly.
  • Stakeholders might be inherently distrustful of salespeople, regardless of your actions, making it difficult to build trust.
  • The pressure to perform and meet quotas can lead salespeople to compromise their ethics and engage in behaviors that erode trust.

Rebuttals

  • Building trust is an investment that can lead to long-term relationships, repeat business, and higher customer lifetime value.
  • While some stakeholders might be initially distrustful, consistent actions and honest communication can overcome their skepticism.
  • Maintaining ethical standards and building trust are essential for long-term success in sales. Short-term gains achieved through unethical tactics will ultimately damage your reputation and erode your credibility.

Discussion Topics

  • The role of vulnerability and authenticity in building trust.
  • The challenges of maintaining trust in high-pressure sales environments.
  • The ethical considerations of using psychological principles to influence stakeholder behavior.

Argument Builder

Supporting Points

  • Trust is essential for building strong relationships, advancing deals, and achieving ultra-high sales performance.
  • By consistently demonstrating trustworthiness through their actions, behaviors, and communication, salespeople can overcome the initial barrier of distrust and create a foundation for a successful relationship.
  • Understanding the concept of being "on stage" and managing your "emotional baggage" can help you project trustworthiness and inspire stakeholder confidence.

Counterpoints

  • Some might argue that trust is earned over time and that salespeople should focus on demonstrating their expertise and closing the deal quickly, rather than trying to build trust from the outset.
  • Stakeholders might be inherently distrustful of salespeople, making it difficult to build trust regardless of your efforts.
  • The pressure to perform can lead salespeople to make promises they can't keep or engage in behaviors that erode trust, making it challenging to maintain ethical standards.

Rebuttals

  • While trust deepens over time, every interaction provides an opportunity to build or erode trust. Focusing on building trust from the beginning sets the stage for a positive relationship and enhances your credibility.
  • While some stakeholders might be initially skeptical, consistent actions, honest communication, and a genuine desire to help can overcome distrust and build a strong foundation for a relationship.
  • Maintaining ethical standards and prioritizing building trust are crucial for long-term success in sales. Short-term gains achieved through unethical tactics will ultimately damage your reputation and career.

Practical Application: Building Trust Through Consistent Actions

Actionable Task 28.1: Keep Your Promises

  • Steps: Be impeccable with your word. Only make promises you can keep, and follow through on every commitment you make, no matter how small.
  • Resources: Calendar, task management tool, accountability partner or coach.
  • Obstacles/Solutions: Overwhelmed by commitments? Prioritize tasks, delegate if possible, and communicate proactively if you encounter delays or obstacles.
  • Timeline: Implement immediately, making it a non-negotiable personal standard.

Actionable Task 28.2: Be Transparent and Honest

  • Steps: Communicate openly and honestly with stakeholders, even when it's difficult. Admit mistakes, address concerns proactively, and avoid exaggerating or making promises you can't keep.
  • Resources: Practice clear and concise communication, role-play challenging conversations with a colleague, seek feedback on your communication style.
  • Obstacles/Solutions: Worried about appearing vulnerable? Remind yourself that honesty and transparency build trust, while deception erodes it.
  • Timeline: Commit to being transparent and honest in all your interactions with stakeholders.

Evaluation

Success is measured by increased stakeholder confidence in your reliability, a reduction in skepticism or questioning of your motives, and a greater willingness to collaborate and share information.
